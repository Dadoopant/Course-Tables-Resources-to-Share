TIER 4

Key Findings: What Explains Performance Variation?

Critical Insight: Risk Level Does NOT Predict Performance

High performers despite extreme risk: San Mateo County (43.3% at risk, $110B regional need), Charleston (50.6% at risk, projected 180 flood days/year by 2045), Miami-Dade (46.9% at risk, sunny-day flooding currently occurring)
Poor performers despite high risk: Norfolk (51.6% at risk, 2nd highest US vulnerability), Honolulu (38% at risk, 4 ft SLR by 2100), Jersey City (35.3% at risk)
Core finding: Governance structure + funding mechanisms matter MORE than risk level for determining performance outcomes

Strong Performers Share These Features:

  • State-level enabling legislation/funding: Florida Resilient Florida program (>$1B statewide), California Coastal Conservancy grants, South Carolina statewide resilience plan (2022)
  • Regional coordination mechanisms: San Mateo OneShoreline (20 cities), SE FL Regional Climate Compact (4 counties), Hampton Roads framework, BCDC Bay Area coordination (9 counties)
  • Federal catalysts: Hurricane Sandy disaster funding (NYC $900M+, Jersey City $230M), military/DoD interest and partnership, USACE cost-sharing partnerships
  • Innovative governance structures: Special districts (OneShoreline), independent port authorities (PA NY/NJ), Chief Resilience Officer positions, multi-agency coordination capacity

Weak Performers Share These Constraints:

  • Fiscal constraints: Stockton (post-bankruptcy recovery takes precedence), Norfolk (adaptation need equals entire annual city budget), Boston (limited revenue tools, no home rule)
  • Fragmented governance without coordination: Honolulu (8 separate plans with no integration), Norfolk (city/state port/federal military split with unclear authority), pre-RSAP Bay Area jurisdictional fragmentation
  • Political barriers: Honolulu Legislature terminology change (2017), variable state political will affecting implementation consistency
  • State control without revenue sharing: Boston Chapter 91 model (state controls and restricts WITHOUT providing funding, coordination, or technical support)

Boston's Unique Governance Challenge

Massachusetts Chapter 91 represents the most constraining governance structure in this entire 14-city comparative analysis.

The California Comparison Reveals the Problem: California cities (San Mateo, Oakland, Long Beach) also have state oversight of waterfront lands via public trust doctrine and Coastal Commission jurisdiction. However, California provides grants (Coastal Conservancy, OPC), regional coordination frameworks (BCDC RSAP), technical assistance, and clear compliance guidance (AB 691). Massachusetts provides control and restrictions WITHOUT corresponding funding, coordination, or technical support. This governance gap—not the existence of state oversight itself—is Boston's primary structural barrier to effective SLR adaptation.

Governance Models Ranked by Effectiveness:

  1. Independent bi-state authority: Port Authority NY/NJ (serves NYC, Jersey City) — Independent revenue streams, mandatory climate guidelines, regional scale
  2. Innovative special district: San Mateo OneShoreline — Crosses 20 jurisdictional boundaries, creates dedicated funding mechanism, solves fragmentation
  3. Strong county-level coordination: Miami-Dade — Regional scale enables multi-city coordination, substantial tax base, aggressive grant pursuit
  4. State partnership enabling local action: Charleston (SC), St. Petersburg (FL) — State provides enabling framework + funding + technical support for local implementation
  5. City-controlled port department: Oakland, Long Beach — Mayor-appointed boards maintain city control, port generates revenue (though insufficient for system-wide needs)
  6. Regional multi-jurisdictional coordination: Hampton Roads (Virginia Beach, Norfolk) — Coordination framework established but fragmented implementation across multiple jurisdictions
  7. University-enhanced municipal capacity: Cambridge — Technical expertise and research capacity from MIT/Harvard, but shares Boston's Chapter 91 constraints
  8. State control without support (LEAST EFFECTIVE): Boston — Commonwealth owns and controls waterfront WITHOUT providing funding, coordination, or technical assistance
  9. Fragmented multi-level without coordinator: Norfolk — City/state port/federal military with no clear primary authority or coordination mechanism
  10. Fiscally constrained post-crisis: Stockton — Post-bankruptcy fiscal recovery limits all climate adaptation spending

Revenue Mechanism Effectiveness:

Most effective funding models:

  • State grant programs with dedicated climate funding (FL Resilient Florida $1B+, CA Coastal Conservancy project grants)
  • Independent authorities with revenue generation (PA NY/NJ port revenue, San Mateo special district taxation)
  • Post-disaster federal catalysts (Hurricane Sandy: NYC $900M+, Jersey City $230M HUD "Resist" program)

Moderately effective:

  • Regional coordination with state support (Hampton Roads RGGI fund, Tampa Bay Regional Planning)
  • Innovative local revenue (Charleston tourism tax 2020 law, Maui 20% to managed retreat fund)
  • County-scale tax base (Miami-Dade combined with aggressive grant pursuit)
  • Port-generated revenue (Oakland, Long Beach—insufficient for system-wide needs but enables some action)

Least effective:

  • State control without revenue sharing (Boston Chapter 91—control WITHOUT funding)
  • Post-bankruptcy constraints (Stockton—fiscal crisis overrides climate priorities)
  • Limited local authority with massive need (Norfolk—adaptation cost equals entire annual city budget)
