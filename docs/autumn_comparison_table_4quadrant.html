Climate Resilience and Residential Land: Comparative Case Analysis

Sea-Level Rise Response Across Wealth and Vulnerability Dimensions | PPUA 5266 Final Project

NYC Basement Apartments
POOR/VULNERABLE — FAILURE
Location & Demographics
Queens and Bronx, NYC. Working-class immigrant neighborhoods (60-80% minority: Latino, Asian, Black). Est. 50,000-100,000 residents in basement units citywide. Median income $40,000-60,000. 90%+ renters. Working-age families (25-55) with children.
Climate Threat
Flash flooding from extreme precipitation. Hurricane Ida (2021): 11 deaths in basement apartments, mostly Queens. Below-grade units with inadequate drainage, illegal construction. FEMA: 100-year flood now 25-year event. Aging infrastructure compounds risk.
Resilience Interventions
MINIMAL TO NONE. Some flood sensors, limited awareness campaigns. No alternative housing. City enforcement would displace 50,000-100,000 residents → political paralysis. Post-Ida: Federal disaster relief but no proactive structural changes.
Key Actors & Power
Landlords: Profit from illegal units, perverse incentive against compliance. City: Democratic administration, avoids housing crisis by tolerating dangerous conditions. Residents: Limited organizing due to immigration status, language barriers. Federal: Post-disaster relief only.
Outcomes
Physical: Zero flood risk reduction. Economic: Landlords continue profiting; tenants bear all risk. Social: 11 deaths; enforcement = displacement of 50,000-100,000; status quo preserved. Sustainability: Risk increasing with climate change; no resolution path. COMPLETE FAILURE
Theoretical Connections
Trounstine: Land-use regulation protects high-value areas while ignoring vulnerable; racist immigration enforcement enables exploitation.
Castells: City planning decisions (Space of Flows) sacrifice immigrant lives (Space of Places) for capital accumulation.
Harvey: Tenants denied right to safe housing; spatial exploitation of vulnerable populations.
Fainstein: Fails all three Just City criteria—equity (deaths concentrated in vulnerable), democracy (no voice for undocumented), diversity (endangered by displacement risk).
The Hamptons Beach Nourishment
RICH/POWERFUL — FAILURE
Location & Demographics
Southampton and East Hampton, Suffolk County, NY. Ultra-wealthy (median income $120,000+, seasonal residents much higher). 85%+ white, non-Hispanic. 70%+ second/vacation homes; high ownership concentration. Older wealthy population (55-75) plus some younger wealthy families. Strong local tax base supplemented by federal flood insurance subsidies.
Climate Threat
Chronic coastal erosion (1-3 feet/year) plus major storm impacts. Barrier island geography, sandy substrate, narrow beach width. Erosion documented since 1930s, worsening since 1990s with sea-level rise acceleration. Beach loss inevitable without perpetual intervention.
Resilience Interventions
Engineering approach: Beach nourishment (pumping sand from offshore), repeated cycles every 3-5 years. Hard structures (groins, seawalls). Property elevation (some). Funding: $30M+ per nourishment cycle (federal ~65%, local ~35%); hundreds of millions cumulative over decades. Multi-level governance: Army Corps of Engineers, local municipalities, private homeowners' associations.
Key Actors & Power
Homeowners/Real Estate: Lobby for continued federal funding despite ineffectiveness. Federal: Army Corps beach nourishment, National Flood Insurance Program subsidizes rebuilding (working class pays via taxes). Local Gov't: Strong but dependent on federal support. State: Coastal Zone Management, beach access disputes (public trust doctrine).
Outcomes
Physical: Sand repeatedly washes away within 3-5 years; hard structures damage adjacent properties; utterly unsustainable. Economic: Property values maintained temporarily but costs spiral; tax burden on working class via federal insurance. Social: No displacement of wealthy; service workers priced out of entire region. Sustainability: Perpetual re-investment required; eventually untenable. EXPENSIVE FAILURE
Theoretical Connections
Molotch: Growth machine attempting to preserve land value when land commodity literally washes away; demonstrates limits when geology defeats capital.
Harvey: Attempted spatial fix creates new contradictions; wealthy assert exclusive right to coastal space with privatized benefits, socialized costs.
Keenan: Not applicable (already wealthy), BUT note broader pattern of federal subsidies enabling unsustainable coastal development.
Fainstein: Fails all Just City criteria—equity (subsidies to wealthy), democracy (elite capture), diversity (exclusionary).
New Orleans Resilience Hubs
POOR/VULNERABLE — SUCCESS?
Location & Demographics
Multiple neighborhoods, New Orleans, LA. City pop. ~370,000 (post-Katrina recovery). Affected neighborhoods: 60% Black, 30% other minorities. Median household income $41,604 citywide (affected areas lower). 55% renters citywide (higher in affected areas). Aging population (many elderly remained post-Katrina).
Climate Threat
Hurricane storm surge plus chronic subsidence and "nuisance flooding." Below-sea-level geography, aging pump systems, subsiding land. Major hurricanes (Betsy 1965, Katrina 2005) plus 50+ smaller events since 2000. Entire city vulnerable; category 3+ hurricane would overtop levees. Regional infrastructure interdependence creates cascading risks.
Resilience Interventions
Social infrastructure approach: Community resilience hubs (dedicated buildings), emergency supply pre-positioning, coordination networks, training programs. Funding: $15M+ from multiple sources—FEMA grants, nonprofit fundraising, city matching funds. Governance: Hybrid model—federal funding, city implementation, community organizations operate hubs. Timeline: Post-Katrina (2005-present); hubs operational ~2023-2025.
Key Actors & Power
Community Orgs: Neighborhood associations, NGOs, faith-based groups, mutual aid networks (legacy of civil rights era organizing). City: Office of Resilience coordinates. Federal: FEMA and HUD Community Development Block Grants. State: Limited capacity but some post-Katrina support.
Outcomes
Physical: Hubs speed aid distribution but don't prevent flooding; pumps still fail regularly. Economic: Avoided worse economic collapse; tourism recovering; BUT many residents never returned. Social: CRITICAL QUESTION—Did hubs enable residents to stay or did area gentrify? Social capital and institutions built. Sustainability: Social capital persists; model replicable. NEEDS VERIFICATION
Theoretical Connections
Fainstein: IF hubs prevented displacement and respected community control: aligns with Just City (equity + democracy + diversity). Community-led model respects local knowledge and dignity.
Harvey: Residents assert right to return and remain post-disaster; challenges displacement logic.
Keenan: KEY TEST—If area gentrified post-hubs, validates climate gentrification model (resilience → property value increase → displacement). If NOT, shows community control can resist gentrification.
Molotch: Post-disaster rebuilding as alternative to abandonment; tension between growth and equity agendas.
Carlsbad Managed Retreat
RICH/POWERFUL — SUCCESS
Location & Demographics
Carlsbad, North San Diego County, CA. City pop. ~115,000. Median household income $105,000+. 65% white, 25% Latino. 60% homeowners. Mix of retirees and working professionals. Strong tax base (~$90M+ annual city budget). Affluent coastal community.
Climate Threat
Coastal erosion from wave action and sea-level rise. Bluffs undermined by waves; unstable geology. Erosion accelerating since 1980s; El Niño winters particularly damaging. Coast Highway threatened within 20-30 years without action. Chronic stress rather than acute disaster.
Resilience Interventions
Planned adaptation: Managed retreat—moving Coast Highway inland rather than fighting erosion. Zoning changes, building setbacks, proactive planning. Funding: $40M+ for highway relocation (Caltrans state transportation, local budget, BRIC federal grants). Timeline: 2020s planning phase; construction projected 2025-2030. City-led with California Coastal Commission approval required.
Key Actors & Power
City: Public Works leads project. State: Caltrans (transportation), Coastal Commission (approval/veto power, extensive environmental review required). Federal: BRIC grant eligibility, potential federal highway funds. Property Owners: Some resistance, lawsuits potential. Tourism Industry: Concerned about beach access impacts.
Outcomes
Physical: Highway will be protected (projected); development setbacks enforced; addresses root cause. Economic: Highway maintenance costs reduced long-term; some property devaluation near coast. Social: Minimal displacement (few residents near highway); concern about future development patterns and service worker housing. Sustainability: One-time infrastructure move avoids perpetual maintenance; sustainable approach. PROVISIONAL SUCCESS
Theoretical Connections
Molotch: Growth machine CAN make rational adaptation choices when resources available; shifts infrastructure to preserve development potential rather than fighting nature.
Harvey: Public infrastructure prioritized over private beachfront property. BUT: Who has "right" to coast? Beachfront owners lose beach access value.
Fainstein: MIXED on Just City criteria—Democracy in process (public planning), equity QUESTIONABLE (who bears costs of lost beach access? Service workers still priced out of housing in area), diversity unclear.
KEY QUESTION: Is this "success" or just wealthy people's problems being solved with public funds while service workers remain vulnerable?
